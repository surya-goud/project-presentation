Executive Summary

The City of Riverton has relied on a legacy parking management system for over a decade a system that was originally built for a much smaller population. As the city grew, the cracks widened. Residents wait up to 4.7 days for basic permit approvals. Enforcement officers patrol with handheld devices that receive permit updates hours late through batch uploads, causing them to cite vehicles whose permits were approved earlier that morning. The result: 28.5% of all appeals filed are approved as wrongful citations a direct cost to community trust and city revenue. Meanwhile, $108,722 in fines go uncollected annually due to disconnected payment systems and no automated follow-up. Duplicate records flood the database because residents resubmit applications with no confirmation that the first was received.

Our team analyzed 50,000 real transactions across three integrated data tables, Transaction, Payment, and Audit Status to identify the root causes of these failures and propose ParkIQ: a unified parking data ecosystem. ParkIQ replaces the batch-upload model with real-time data sync across all four source systems, delivers role-specific dashboards to four types of stakeholders, and provides enforcement officers with instant permit verification before issuing any citation. Based on our simulation, implementing ParkIQ projects a 96% reduction in processing time, an 89% drop in sync failures, and $86K in annual revenue recovery.

Research & Data Design

Dataset Architecture

Three interconnected tables built from real Riverton parking data — 150,000 total records across 40 fields, designed to mirror the four source systems the city currently uses.

📋
Transaction_Table
50,000 rows · 18 columns
Transaction_ID · Vehicle_ID · Permit_ID · Parking_Space_ID · Zone_ID · Zone_Name · Zone_Type · Event_Time · Permit_Start_Time · Permit_End_Time · Permit_Status · Occupancy_Status · Violation_Type · Violation_Status · Fine_Amount
Key design rule: Violation_Type is empty for 38,599 records (no violation) — only 11,401 rows have a violation. Fines are only assigned when a violation exists.
💳
Payment_Table
50,000 rows · 9 columns
Payment_ID · Transaction_ID · Fine_Amount · Payment_Amount · Payment_Status · Payment_Date · Due_Date · Payment_Method
Key design rule: Payment_Status = "No Fine" for 38,599 records. Only Paid (9,188), Unpaid (1,684) and Partial (529) records have actual fine amounts. Payment_Method = "—" for unfined records.
🔍
Audit_Status_Table
50,000 rows · 13 columns
Audit_ID · Transaction_ID · Appeal_ID · Appeal_Status · Appeal_Reason · Appeal_Date · Data_Source_System · Data_Sync_Status · Sensor_Occupancy_Status · Record_Status · Sensor_ID · Sync_Time
Key design rule: Data_Sync_Status is exactly "True" or "False" — 46,489 True (93%), 3,511 False (7%). Appeal_Status = "None" for 49,084 records; only 916 have active appeals.

Key Insights from the Dashboard

🔴 Expired Meter Leads at 27.8%

The Violation Type Distribution shows Expired Meter generating 3,165 citations — nearly 1,000 more than the next type. This reveals a real-time information gap: drivers lack live meter status and officers have no tools to distinguish an expired meter from a recent payment. Both problems are solved by live data integration.

🔴 28.5% Appeals Approved = Wrongful Citations

The Appeals Overview waterfall shows 261 Approved, 318 Pending, 337 Rejected — total 916. An approval rate of 28.5% is critically high. It directly traces to the 0.93 Sync Rate KPI: officers cite residents with valid permits that haven't appeared on their devices yet due to 3,511 sync failures.

🔵 Z005 & Z007 Carry Highest Risk

Transactions by Zone shows Riverfront (Z005) at 6,387 and Student Housing (Z007) at 6,340 as the two highest-volume zones. High footfall with enforcement data lag creates concentrated violation hotspots. These zones need priority real-time coverage in any solution design.

🟡 91% Occupancy · Only 0.18 Compliance

Sensor Occupancy shows 45,028 occupied spaces (91.25%) — zones are nearly full. Yet Payment Compliance is only 0.18. High usage is not translating into revenue. The Violations by Payment Status chart confirms: 39K records carry "No Fine". Revenue is being generated but not captured by the disconnected payment pipeline.

Key Features & How They Solve the Problem

📊
Command Center Dashboard

Live KPIs computed from all 3 CSV tables in real-time. Monthly violation trends, zone volume, sync health and payment compliance — all visible in one view without switching between departments or spreadsheets.

Solves: Lack of unified operational visibility for management
🔄
Real-Time Sync Monitor

Tracks Data_Sync_Status per source system — Enforcement App, Sensor Network, Permit Portal, Payment Gateway. Officers receive permit updates in under 30 seconds instead of hours. Force-sync action available for immediate resolution.

Solves: 3,511 sync failures causing 261 wrongful citations
⚡
Officer Permit Lookup

Before issuing any citation, enforcement officers enter a Vehicle ID and receive instant verification against all 50,000 records — active permit, expiry date, zone, and permit type shown immediately. Green = valid, Red = expired/invalid.

Solves: Wrongful citation rate of 28.5% at the source
⚖️
Appeal Management

All 916 real appeal records are filterable by status and reason. One-click Approve or Reject updates in-memory data instantly. Filters isolate all 318 pending cases so staff can process them in one focused session.

Solves: 318 pending appeals with no centralized workflow
📋
Permit Management

Filterable permit queue showing all 50,000 transaction records. Filter by status (Active/Expired/Suspended), zone, violation type, or vehicle ID. New Permit modal includes instant duplicate detection against all existing records.

Solves: Duplicate records and manual permit processing
🗺️
Zone Occupancy Monitoring

Sensor_Occupancy_Status data visualized per zone — 8 tiles showing occupancy %, risk level, and transaction volume. Identifies near-capacity zones (Z005: 91%, Z007: 88%) for proactive patrol routing decisions.

Solves: Inconsistent enforcement across zones

Ethical & Social Considerations

Responsible Data Use

Modernizing a city system means handling sensitive resident data, enforcement records, and financial information. ParkIQ was designed with these responsibilities in mind.

🔒
Data Privacy

Vehicle IDs and permit records are sensitive data tied to individual residents. ParkIQ implements role-based access — enforcement officers can only see what they need to do their job. Finance staff cannot access citation details unrelated to payment collection.

⚖️
Algorithmic Fairness

Automated enforcement tools risk amplifying existing biases. Zone-level data shows enforcement volume varies across neighborhoods. Any deployment must be audited regularly to ensure citation patterns are driven by actual violations — not patrol routing preferences.

🤝
Community Trust

The 261 wrongful citations in our dataset represent 261 real interactions where residents lost trust in the city. The Permit Lookup tool directly addresses this by making wrongful citations structurally impossible — rebuilding the city-resident relationship through transparency.

📱
Digital Equity

Not all residents have equal access to online systems. Any digitization of permit processes must retain alternative channels for residents without reliable internet access. The payment portal should support in-person and phone payment options alongside the digital experience.

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

What We Learned

01
Data quality is not a technical problem — it's an organizational one

The 1,488 duplicate records didn't happen because of a database bug. They happened because residents got no confirmation after submitting. Fixing the technical sync issue requires understanding the human behavior driving it.

02
One root cause can explain multiple symptoms

We initially treated sync failures, wrongful citations, and appeals as three separate problems. Through data analysis, we discovered they are all caused by the same thing: batch-upload architecture. Solving sync solved all three.

03
Cross-table analysis reveals what single-table reports miss

Neither the Transaction Table nor the Audit Table alone shows the full picture. Joining them via Transaction_ID reveals which specific sync failures caused which specific wrongful citations — the kind of insight that drives targeted action.

04
Dashboard design is a communication skill

Knowing what the data shows is only half the work. The harder part is deciding what to highlight, in what order, and for which audience. Designing four different role views forced us to think about what each stakeholder actually needs to decide.

Next Steps

1
Phase 1: Real-time sync API — Replace batch upload with webhook-based permit sync. Target: sub-30-second update latency for all officer devices.
2
Phase 2: Automated payment reminders — Send SMS/email to unpaid accounts at 7, 14, and 30 days. Projected to recover $86K annually from the current $108K uncollected gap.
3
Phase 3: Predictive patrol routing — Use zone occupancy and historical violation data to recommend patrol schedules. Reduce reactive enforcement in favor of data-driven coverage.
4
Phase 4: Resident self-service portal — Online permit applications with real-time status updates and instant confirmation — eliminating the resubmission behavior causing 1,488 duplicates.
