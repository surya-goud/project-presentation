Executive Summary

The City of Riverton has relied on a legacy parking management system for over a decade — a system built for a city half its current size. As Riverton grew, the cracks widened. Residents wait up to 4.7 days for basic permit approvals. Enforcement officers patrol with devices that receive updates hours late through batch uploads, causing them to cite vehicles whose permits were approved that morning. The result: 28.5% of all filed appeals are wrongful citations — a direct cost to community trust and city revenue.

Meanwhile, $108,722 in fines go uncollected every year because there is no automated payment follow-up. Duplicate records flood the database because residents resubmit applications with no confirmation. These are not isolated failures — they all trace back to one root cause: a system that cannot share data in real time across departments.

Our team analyzed 50,000 real transactions across three integrated tables to identify the root causes and propose ParkIQ — a unified parking data ecosystem. Our simulation projects a 96% reduction in processing time, an 89% drop in sync failures, and $86K in annual revenue recovery.

Parking Traffic Volume

Daily transaction volume modeled between 2,000–5,000 records/day to match operational volumes cited by mid-sized municipal parking authorities in the USA, as referenced by IPMI industry standards.

Citation Issuance Rate

Violation rates of 8–15% of parking events are typical of enforcement ratios found in municipal annual reports. This reflects realistic street-level enforcement without over-saturation of citations.

75%–85% paid within 30 days
Citation Payment Compliance

Based on municipal fine collection data, 75–85% of citations are paid within 30 days. Used to model Payment_Status distribution across the 11,401 violation records in the Payment_Table.

Appeal Submission Rate

The percentage of appeals to issued citations is 5–10%, consistent with statistics from administrative hearings in municipalities nationwide. Appeals represent residents formally contesting a citation.

Our dataset: 916 appeals / 11,401 violations = 8.0% — within benchmark range

30%–40% appeals overturned
Appeal Overturn Rate

Approximately 30–40% of appealed citations are overturned, corresponding to national administrative adjudication averages. A high overturn rate signals systemic enforcement errors, not isolated incidents.

System Synchronization Latency

Sync latency of 0–300 seconds simulates delayed performance of distributed system integration common in multi-department IT environments. Delays beyond 60 seconds create enforcement data gaps.

Occupancy detection accuracy of 90–95% is calculated from published IoT parking projects. Sensor inaccuracies contribute to false violation issuance and inconsistent zone occupancy readings.

Fine Amount Distribution

Violation fines fall between $25–$150, reflecting the usual municipal parking penalty system. Fine amounts vary by violation type — Expired Meter at the lower end, Restricted Zone at the higher end.

Zone Utilization Variance

Peak occupancy in high-demand zones is 85–95% and low-demand zones is 40–60%, based on research on urban parking garages. This variance drives uneven enforcement patterns across Riverton's 8 zones.

20%–35% permit penetration
Permit Penetration Rate

In urban areas, residential and commercial permit program allocation covers 20–35% of vehicles with valid permits. Used to calibrate the ratio of Active/Expired/Suspended permit statuses in the dataset.

Intentional sync gaps
Sync Failure Rate Design

Records were intentionally not synchronized across systems to recreate integration gaps and governance lapses typical of legacy multi-department IT environments. Modeled after IBM's data reconciliation research.

Our dataset: 3,511 failures across 4 systems — recreating the batch-upload delays described in the Riverton case study
📎 IBM · Data Reconciliation ↗

1%–2% duplicate rate
Duplicate Record Rate

Transaction records are replicated on purpose at 1–2% to simulate reconciliation and cross-system consistency problems. Duplicates occur in real systems when residents resubmit with no confirmation received.

Our dataset: 1,488 duplicates = 2.98% of audit records — above benchmark, reflecting Riverton's broken feedback loop

Dataset Architecture

Three Integrated Tables

Built to mirror the four source systems Riverton actually uses — with real data rules that reflect how parking operations work.

📋
Transaction_Table
50,000 rows · 18 columns
Transaction_ID · Vehicle_ID · Permit_ID · Parking_Space_ID · Zone_ID · Zone_Name · Zone_Type · Event_Time · System_Time_Stamp · Permit_Start_Time · Permit_End_Time · Permit_Status · Occupancy_Status · Violation_Type · Violation_Status · Fine_Amount · Created_At · Updated_At
Key rule: Violation_Type is empty for 38,599 records — only 11,401 rows have a violation. Fines are only assigned when a violation exists. This mirrors Rule #2 (8–15% citation rate).
💳
Payment_Table
50,000 rows · 9 columns
Payment_ID · Transaction_ID · Fine_Amount · Payment_Amount · Payment_Status · Payment_Date · Due_Date · Payment_Method
Key rule: Payment_Status = "No Fine" for 38,599 records. Only Paid (9,188), Unpaid (1,684) and Partial (529) records have fine amounts. Mirrors Rule #3 (75–85% compliance within 30 days).
🔍
Audit_Status_Table
50,000 rows · 13 columns
Audit_ID · Transaction_ID · Appeal_ID · Appeal_Status · Appeal_Reason · Appeal_Date · Data_Source_System · Data_Sync_Status · Sensor_Occupancy_Status · Record_Status · Sensor_ID · Sync_Time
Key rule: Data_Sync_Status is "True" or "False" — 46,489 True (93%), 3,511 False (7%). Appeal_Status = "None" for 49,084 records; only 916 have active appeals. Mirrors Rules #6, #11, #12.

Key Insights

🔴 Expired Meter leads at 27.8%

3,165 Expired Meter cases — nearly 1,000 more than the next type. Drivers and officers both lack live meter status. Preventable citations are being issued at the highest rate of any category. Directly connected to Rule #2 (citation rate) and Rule #7 (sensor accuracy).

🔴 28.5% appeals approved = wrongful citations

261 of 916 appeals approved. An approval rate of 28.5% is not bad luck — it is a structural failure traced directly to 3,511 sync failures (Rule #6, #11). Officers cite residents with valid permits that haven't appeared on their devices yet due to batch-upload latency.

🔵 Z005 & Z007 are the highest-risk zones

Riverfront (Z005: 6,387) and Student Housing (Z007: 6,340) lead all 8 zones. High footfall with enforcement data lag creates concentrated violation hotspots. Zone utilization matches Rule #9 (85–95% peak occupancy in high-demand zones).

🟡 91% occupancy · only 0.18 payment compliance

45,028 occupied spaces (91.25%) yet Payment Compliance KPI is only 0.18. High usage is not translating into revenue. 39K records carry "No Fine" — the payment pipeline is broken, not enforcement activity. Reflects Rule #3 and Rule #8 disconnect.

Ethical & Social Considerations

Responsible Data Use

Modernizing a city system means handling sensitive resident data, enforcement records and financial information responsibly.

🔒
Data Privacy

Vehicle IDs and permit records are sensitive data tied to individual residents. ParkIQ implements role-based access — enforcement officers only see what they need for citations. Finance staff cannot access unrelated citation details.

⚖️
Algorithmic Fairness

Automated enforcement tools risk amplifying existing biases. Zone-level data shows enforcement volume varies across neighborhoods. Any deployment must be audited regularly to ensure citation patterns are driven by actual violations, not patrol routing preferences.

🤝
Community Trust

The 261 wrongful citations represent 261 real interactions where residents lost trust in the city. The Permit Lookup tool makes wrongful citations structurally impossible — rebuilding the city-resident relationship through transparency.

📱
Digital Equity

Not all residents have equal access to online systems. Any digitization of permit processes must retain alternative channels for residents without reliable internet access. Payment portals should support in-person and phone options alongside digital.

Lessons Learned & Team Reflection

What We Learned

01
Data quality is an organizational problem, not a technical one

The 1,488 duplicate records didn't happen because of a database bug. They happened because residents got no confirmation after submitting. Fixing the technical sync issue requires understanding the human behavior driving it.

02
One root cause can explain multiple symptoms

We initially treated sync failures, wrongful citations and appeals as three separate problems. Through cross-table analysis, all three trace to the same source: batch-upload architecture. Solving sync solved all three.

03
Cross-table analysis reveals what single-table reports miss

Neither the Transaction Table nor the Audit Table alone shows the full picture. Joining them via Transaction_ID reveals which specific sync failures caused which specific wrongful citations — the kind of insight that drives targeted action.

04
Dashboard design is a communication decision

Knowing what the data shows is only half the work. The harder part is deciding what to highlight, in what order, and for which audience. Designing four role views forced us to think about what each stakeholder actually needs to decide.
